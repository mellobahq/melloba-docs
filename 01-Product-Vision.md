## Founder's Note

I am writing this at the beginning, before there is a product to defend or a number to protect, because I want the reasons I started Melloba to be legible long after the day-to-day has buried them.

I did not start Melloba because I noticed a gap in a market. I started it because of a quiet realization that would not leave me alone: the single most formative environment in a human life — the everyday conversation between a young child and the people who love them — is also the least supported one we have. We instrument almost everything else. We track our steps, our sleep, our spending, the performance of systems that matter far less than this. But the thousands of small exchanges that actually build a child's mind happen in the dark, unnoticed even by the people having them. That struck me as both strange and correctable.

I believe everyday conversation is one of the highest-leverage investments a person can make in a child's future, and I do not mean that as a sentiment. The evidence points to something specific and, to me, quietly astonishing: it is not the volume of words a child hears that shapes them most, but the back-and-forth — the turns, the responses, the being genuinely talked *with* rather than *at*. These moments are free. They require no wealth, no curriculum, no device. What they require is attention and intention, and intention is exactly the thing that a little visibility can strengthen. A parent who can see what they already do well, and take one small next step, is more powerful than any program I could build to replace them. Melloba's entire bet is that we can hand parents that visibility without turning love into a scoreboard.

I am committing years to this, not months, and I want to be honest about why. The things worth building here — trust with families around their most intimate data, evidence that we actually help, a company that cannot betray the people who rely on it even if someone later wanted it to — none of these can be rushed. Trust compounds slowly and collapses quickly. I would rather build something that earns the right to exist over a decade than something that wins a year and is rightly forgotten. If that is too slow for a given quarter, then the quarter is wrong, not the plan.

This matters to me beyond building a successful business, and I think the distinction is important enough to write down. A profitable company that made parents more anxious, or treated children's voices as a resource to be mined, would be a failure I would not want my name on — regardless of its valuation. What I actually want is narrower and larger than success: that some number of children, because the adults around them became a little more intentional, grow up in richer conversation than they otherwise would have. If Melloba does that and never becomes large, I will consider it worth the years. If it becomes large and does not do that, I will consider it a mistake I made carefully.

Everything after this page is the discipline required to make the first outcome likely and the second impossible. Read the rest not as promises but as constraints I am choosing, in advance, to hold myself to.

— Simanta

## Vision Statement

**A world where every child grows up surrounded by rich, responsive conversation — and every parent can see, and shape, the difference it makes.**

---

## Mission Statement

**Melloba helps parents understand and improve their everyday conversations with their children — turning ordinary moments into deliberate development, privately and without judgment.**

---

## Motto

**Talk *with*, not just *to*.**

This is our primary motto, and it is more than a phrase — it is a decision test. It compresses the strongest finding in the science (that contingent, back-and-forth conversation is what builds a child's mind) and our core product philosophy (that we help parents *engage*, not merely *produce more speech*) into five words a new engineer, designer, or marketer can apply on their first day. If a feature nudges a parent to talk *with* their child, it is probably right; if it nudges them to perform *at* their child, it is probably wrong.

Alternatives considered:

- *"Trust before scale."* — True and important, but it describes our operating discipline, not our purpose. Better as an internal value (see Core Values) than as the motto.
- *"Build the decade, not the download."* — A sharp reminder against short-termism, but inward-facing; it says more about us than about the child we serve.
- *"Every conversation counts."* — Warm and memorable, but drifts toward marketing and risks implying that quantity is what matters — the exact trap §3 warns against.

*"Talk with, not just to"* wins because it is simultaneously the science, the product thesis, and a usable test for decisions — and because it keeps the child, not the company, at the center.

## 0. How to use this document

This is not a pitch. It is a decision-making instrument. It exists to answer, years from now, a single recurring question: *"Given who we are, what should we do?"*

A good vision document is falsifiable in spirit. It does not just say what we love; it says what we will refuse, what would prove us wrong, and what we are willing to lose in order to stay who we are. If this document could be pasted onto a competitor's website without anyone noticing, it has failed. Read it that way.

Three tests we apply to everything below:

1. **Would this still be true if it became inconvenient?** (Principles that only hold when they're free are not principles.)
2. **Does this help someone make a real decision?** (If it cannot resolve an actual trade-off, it is decoration.)
3. **Would a thoughtful skeptic sign it?** (Not a fan — a skeptic.)

## 1. Why Melloba exists

**Melloba exists to help parents build their children's minds through everyday conversation.**

Most of what shapes a young child's cognitive and emotional development does not happen in a classroom, an app, or a therapy session. It happens in thousands of small, ordinary exchanges with the people who love them — at dinner, in the car, at bedtime, in the ten minutes before school. These moments are the highest-leverage, most under-supported, and most invisible learning environment in a human life.

The people running this environment — parents — are almost entirely unsupported. They receive no feedback, no training, and no visibility into whether the way they talk with their child is helping them flourish or quietly missing opportunities. A parent will get more data about their car's tire pressure than about the single most consequential thing they do as a parent.

Melloba's reason for existing is to close that gap: to give parents gentle, private, evidence-based insight into their own conversations with their children, so that ordinary interaction becomes a deliberate act of development — without turning warmth into a performance metric.

That last clause is not a caveat. It is half the mission. (See §7.)

---

## 2. The fundamental human problem

Strip away the product and the technology. The underlying human problem is this:

> **Parents love their children and want to help them thrive, but they are flying blind through the most formative period of their child's life.**

This blindness has three layers:

**a) The invisibility of the everyday.** The interactions that matter most are precisely the ones that feel too small to notice. No one remembers the 40th conversation of the day. Yet development is the *accumulation* of those 40 conversations, repeated across years.

**b) The absence of feedback.** In almost every domain where humans improve — sport, music, work — improvement depends on feedback. Parenting the early years offers almost none, and the feedback that does exist (a milestone chart at a pediatrician's office, a comparison with a neighbor's child) tends to arrive late, be crude, and provoke anxiety rather than action.

**c) The confidence–competence gap.** Parents are flooded with contradictory advice and marketing, most of it optimized for guilt and engagement rather than for the child. They cannot tell what actually works. The result is a strange combination of anxiety and inertia.

Melloba is a response to this specific problem: not "children don't learn enough," but "the adults who most want to help them cannot see what's happening or what to do next."

---

## 3. Why this problem matters

We ground the company in evidence, not sentiment — and the evidence is stronger, and more specific, than the popular version of it.

**What the science actually supports.** The most robust modern finding is not that children need to *hear more words*. It is that children need to *engage in more back-and-forth conversation*. In a landmark MIT/Harvard study (Romeo et al., 2018), differences in the number of **conversational turns** — the alternation of talk between adult and child — predicted differences in children's language ability *and* in the physical activity of Broca's area, a brain region central to language. The effect held even after accounting for socioeconomic status and sheer quantity of words. Follow-up work (Romeo et al., 2021) reinforced that interactive, contingent conversation is associated with language-related brain function and executive skills.

The researcher's own summary is the clearest statement of our thesis:

> *"The important thing is not just to talk to your child, but to talk with your child."* — Rachel Romeo

**What the science does not support — and why we must be careful.** Melloba must **not** be built on the popular "30 million word gap" (Hart & Risley, 1995). That figure — that poorer children hear 30 million fewer words by age three — has become an education myth. It was extrapolated from one hour a month with 42 families, counted only speech addressed directly to the child, and has since **failed to replicate**: when ambient speech is included, the socioeconomic differences shrink or disappear (Sperry et al., 2018). The concept is also criticized as culturally loaded — treating the speech patterns of some communities as deficits.

**This distinction is a founding decision, not a footnote.** It determines what we measure, how we talk to parents, and what kind of company we are:

- We optimize for **interaction quality (turns, responsiveness, contingency)**, not word *volume*. A product that tells parents to "talk more" is measuring the wrong thing and may worsen the experience for the child.
- We reject a **deficit framing**. Melloba does not tell parents their family is behind. It helps every parent build on what they already do.
- We stay **epistemically honest**: developmental science is probabilistic and contested. We will represent it as such, and we will update as it updates.

Why it matters, in one line: **the early conversational environment is among the highest-leverage inputs into a human life, and it is currently unsupported.** A company that improves it — carefully, honestly, and at scale — is doing something that compounds for decades.

## 4. Why AI makes this possible now

This company could not have existed five years ago, and its timing is not incidental.

1. **Speech understanding is finally good enough.** Reliable, low-cost transcription of natural, messy, multi-speaker, child-and-adult household speech — including a young child's imperfect articulation — has only recently become feasible. This is the enabling technology; without it there is no product.

2. **Language models can analyze interaction, not just words.** The insight from §3 — that *turns and contingency* matter more than volume — is exactly the kind of structure modern models can detect: who spoke, whether a response was contingent on what came before, whether a question was open or closed, whether the adult expanded on the child's utterance. Counting words was always easy; understanding conversation was not. Now it is becoming possible.

3. **Personalization at zero marginal cost.** Generic parenting advice is abundant and nearly worthless. Advice grounded in *this* family's *actual* conversations, delivered privately and specifically, has never been economically possible to produce for millions of families. AI collapses that cost to near zero — which is the entire basis of Melloba's scalability and its business model.

4. **On-device and privacy-preserving computation is maturing.** The single greatest risk to this company (§7, §8) is trust. The same wave of AI progress is making it increasingly possible to process deeply sensitive audio close to the edge, minimizing what must ever leave a family's device. Our timing lets us treat privacy as an architectural choice, not a bolt-on.

**A necessary caution.** "AI makes it possible now" is also the most dangerous sentence a founder can believe. It is true for capability and false for trust, regulation, and social license. The technology is ready before society is. Our job is to move at the speed of *earned trust*, which is slower than the speed of capability. (See §7.)

## 5. The company we intend to become (10-year view)

We describe the destination in terms of *what would be true*, not slogans.

**Year 1–2 — Earn the right to listen.** A focused, exceptional product that does one thing with unusual integrity: help a parent understand and improve their conversations with their child. Success here is not scale; it is trust and genuine, demonstrable helpfulness for a small number of families who would be upset if Melloba disappeared.

**Year 3–5 — Become the trusted layer for developmental insight.** Melloba becomes the private, parent-controlled system of record for a child's conversational and language development over time — the tool a parent opens not in panic but as a habit, and would recommend to a friend precisely *because* it never made them feel judged. Revenue becomes predictable and recurring because the product is genuinely, repeatedly useful.

**Year 5–10 — Institutional trust.** Melloba is credible enough that pediatricians, speech-language professionals, and educators regard its insights as legitimate — not because we replaced them, but because we made their guidance continuous instead of episodic. If we reach this point, we are also positioned to help detect developmental concerns *early*, when intervention is most effective. This is the most valuable and the most dangerous thing we could ever do (§7), and we will not do it until we can do it responsibly.

**What kind of company, in character:**

- A company that would rather grow slowly and be trusted than grow fast and be feared.
- A company that treats children as people to be protected, never as a data resource to be exploited.
- A company small enough in ego to say "the science is uncertain here" and large enough in ambition to still act.
- A company that could be acquired (§11) without the acquirer being able to betray the families who trusted us — because the protections are structural, not cultural.

## 6. Who we serve

**Primary user:** the parent or primary caregiver of a young child (roughly the pre-school and early-school years, where conversational development is most plastic). The parent is our customer, our user, and the party who must be in control of the data.

**Ultimate beneficiary:** the child — who is *not* our user and cannot consent, and whose interests therefore constrain us even when they are inconvenient for growth or revenue. When the parent's convenience and the child's wellbeing conflict, the child's wellbeing governs the product's design.

**Deliberately not our customer (for now):** schools, therapists, advertisers, data brokers, researchers, or the state. Some of these could someday be *partners* on the parent's terms (§5, §11). None of them are the party we answer to. Melloba works for the family.

A tension we name openly: our beneficiary cannot consent and our customer profits emotionally from reassurance. That combination is exactly where child-focused products go wrong — optimizing parental anxiety instead of child outcomes. Guarding against our own incentives is a permanent design constraint, not a one-time decision.

## 7. Principles we will never compromise

These are ranked. When two principles conflict, the higher one wins. This ordering is itself a decision.

**1. The child's wellbeing outranks everything — including the parent's wishes and our revenue.** We do not build features that would benefit the parent or the business at the child's expense (e.g., surveillance framing, pressure to "perform" conversations, anything that makes a child feel measured).

**2. Privacy is existential, not a feature.** We are asking families to let us hear the most intimate audio in their lives — a child's voice. That trust is the whole company. We therefore minimize what we collect, keep processing as close to the device as feasible, never sell or share family data, never train shared models on identifiable family audio without explicit, revocable, informed consent, and design so that even *we* cannot casually access raw family conversations. If a growth tactic requires weakening this, we forgo the growth.

**3. Honesty over reassurance.** We will not manufacture anxiety to drive engagement, nor false comfort to drive retention. We represent uncertain science as uncertain. We would rather a parent close the app calm and informed than hooked and afraid.

**4. Support, never judgment.** Melloba is a coach, not an examiner. It builds on what a parent already does well before it suggests anything. It never produces a "score" that reduces a relationship to a number, and never uses deficit or comparison framing against other children.

**5. Evidence over vibes.** Claims about child development must be traceable to credible evidence, held provisionally, and updated when the evidence changes. Marketing may not outrun the science.

**6. Warmth is not a metric.** We will not let measurement corrode the thing being measured. A relationship optimized like a KPI is damaged. The product must make conversation *more* natural, never more self-conscious.

**7. Longevity over hype.** We optimize for the family who will still trust us in ten years, not the install we can win this quarter.

If we are ever tempted to break one of these, the correct response is to stop, not to rationalize. A documented decision to violate a principle (§12) is the only legitimate path — and it should be rare and painful.

## Core Values

The principles in §7 are the *rules* the company obeys. These values are the *character* the company hires for, promotes for, and behaves by. They are how a Melloba person acts when no rule covers the situation — which is most of the time. They are chosen to reinforce §7 without merely restating it.

**1. Talk with, not just to.**
*What it means:* We engage with the people we serve — parents, children, teammates, users — rather than broadcasting at them. We listen for the contingent reply, not the loudest one.
*Why it matters:* It is our motto and our thesis; a company that talks *at* its users will inevitably build a product that talks *at* children. Our internal behavior leaks into the product.
*In a decision:* When designing feedback for parents, we choose a two-way, responsive coaching interaction over a one-way report card — even though the report card is cheaper to build and easier to demo.

**2. Trust before scale.**
*What it means:* We treat trust as the thing we are actually accumulating, and growth as a by-product of it. We will slow down to keep a promise.
*Why it matters:* Our entire business rests on families letting us hear their children's voices. Trust is the moat, the product, and the exit value; spend it and there is nothing underneath.
*In a decision:* Offered a growth channel that requires broader data collection or a weaker consent flow, we decline it and grow slower, rather than quietly widening our data practices to hit a number.

**3. Honest about what we don't know.**
*What it means:* We state uncertainty out loud, represent contested science as contested, and change our minds in public when evidence changes. We would rather be caught being unsure than caught being wrong-but-confident.
*Why it matters:* We make claims about children's development to anxious parents. Overclaiming is not just a marketing sin here; it can cause real harm. Intellectual honesty is a safety feature.
*In a decision:* Rather than label a child "behind," an early product surfaces observed patterns and explicitly notes the limits of what a few recordings can tell you — even though a confident verdict would feel more "valuable."

**4. Restraint is a feature.**
*What it means:* We treat *not* building as a legitimate, often superior, outcome. We say no to good ideas that are wrong for us, and we keep the product focused on purpose.
*Why it matters:* Almost every way this product goes wrong (surveillance, gamification, scores, anxiety-farming) arrives disguised as a reasonable growth feature. Discipline at the point of "yes" is our main defense.
*In a decision:* Asked to add streaks and rewards to lift retention, we decline on principle (§8) and instead improve the thing that earns retention honestly — genuine usefulness — even though the mechanics would likely bump the metric next quarter.

**5. Build for the child, then for the decade.**
*What it means:* We optimize for the ultimate beneficiary (the child) and for durability, not for the quarter. We make choices we would defend to a parent in ten years.
*Why it matters:* The benefits and harms of what we build compound over years and reach someone who never consented. Short-term thinking is not just suboptimal here; it is a way to hurt people slowly.
*In a decision:* Facing a choice between clean, well-documented, privacy-preserving architecture (slower now) and a fast integration that ships intimate audio to a convenient third party, we pay the upfront cost to protect the child and the company's future (§11, Appendix A.5).

These five are deliberately behavioral. A candidate can be brilliant and still be a poor fit if they cannot hold them; a decision can be locally optimal and still be wrong if it breaks one.

## 8. What we will deliberately NOT build

Non-goals are strategy. Saying no is how a vision survives contact with opportunity. These are prohibitions, not merely "later."

- **No advertising business, ever.** An ad-funded model of children's conversation is a betrayal by design. This is not a phase decision; it is permanent.
- **No selling, brokering, or third-party sharing of family data.** Including "anonymized" or "aggregated" data used in ways families wouldn't expect.
- **No surveillance framing.** Melloba is not a tool for parents to monitor, record, or police their children. It is a tool to help the parent grow. Anything that positions the child as a subject under watch is out.
- **No scores, rankings, or leaderboards for children.** No comparing one child to another. No "your child is in the Nth percentile" as a growth lever.
- **No engagement-maximizing mechanics.** No gamification, streaks-as-manipulation, guilt notifications, or dark patterns. (Note: this includes the gamification and rewards ideas currently excluded from the MVP — we are stating that this exclusion is *principled*, not just a scoping decision.)
- **No medical or diagnostic claims we are not qualified and licensed to make.** Early-signal detection (§5) may become possible, but Melloba will not present itself as a diagnostic authority, replace clinicians, or deliver a life-altering label through an app without appropriate clinical and regulatory grounding.
- **No expansion into community, social sharing, marketplace, or content-library products** *as a way to juice growth* while the core promise is still unproven. These are excluded in the MVP; this document states that they should stay excluded until the core is genuinely trusted and excellent, and even then only if they serve the child — not because they are obvious SaaS growth levers.
- **No feature that requires a child to change their behavior for the product's benefit.** The child is never asked to work for Melloba.

The general rule: **if a feature would make us money by making families anxious, competitive, watched, or exposed, we do not build it — regardless of the revenue.**

## 9. What success means (beyond revenue)

Revenue is necessary. It is not the definition of success; it is the *fuel* for it, and a lagging indicator of whether we are genuinely useful. We hold two scorecards.

**Mission scorecard (why we exist):**
- Parents report — and behavior shows — more and richer *conversational turns* with their children over time.
- Parents feel *more competent and less anxious* after using Melloba, not more. (We will measure this directly and treat a rise in anxiety as a product failure, not a growth signal.)
- Families trust us with their most sensitive data and keep choosing to — measured by durable retention that is *not* engineered through manipulation.
- Where possible and ethical, evidence that children of engaged Melloba families show real developmental benefit.
- Zero serious breaches of the §7 principles.

**Business scorecard (whether we can continue):**
- Predictable recurring revenue from families who would be genuinely unhappy to lose the product.
- Retention driven by usefulness, not lock-in.
- Unit economics that let us keep the privacy promises above without needing to break them to survive.

**The tie-breaker.** If the two scorecards ever point in opposite directions — if the growth-optimal move is the mission-corrosive move — the mission scorecard wins, and we find another way to make the business work. A version of Melloba that hits ₹5 crore by betraying §7 is not a success. It is a failure that happens to be solvent.

We state the financial ambition plainly so it is not mistaken for indifference: we intend to build a durable, valuable business capable of reaching significant scale (on the order of the multi-crore ambition in the founding brief). We simply refuse to reach it by the forbidden paths.

## North Star Metric

A company becomes what it measures. If we point the whole organization at the wrong number, no amount of good intention in this document will save us — the metric will quietly overrule the mission. So the single number we elevate above the others must *be* the mission, expressed as something we can observe.

**Our North Star: the number of families showing sustained improvement in the quality of their conversations with their child over time — measured primarily by growth in genuine back-and-forth engagement (conversational turns and responsive, contingent exchanges), not word volume.**

Read carefully, this metric encodes several of our commitments at once. It counts *families*, not sessions — it cares about people, not usage. It requires *sustained* improvement, so a one-time spike doesn't count. It measures *the parent's behavior with the child*, which is the lever we can ethically move, rather than the child's output, which we refuse to turn into a score (§8). And it is grounded in the defensible science of §3 — turns and contingency — not the contested word-count frame.

**The mandatory guardrail: parental anxiety must not rise.** The North Star is paired with a counter-metric that can veto it. If conversational engagement goes up while parents report feeling more judged, more anxious, or more like they are performing, we have *not* succeeded — we have built the very thing §7.3 and §7.6 forbid. A gain in the North Star bought with a rise in anxiety is disqualified. This pairing is non-negotiable; the North Star may never be reported without its guardrail.

**Why not downloads, MAU, or DAU?** These measure our reach and our habit-forming power, not our helpfulness. A child-conversation product can run all three of these numbers up by manufacturing anxiety and compulsion — exactly the dark patterns we have prohibited. High DAU could even be a *warning sign* that we are cultivating dependence rather than competence. We will of course watch these numbers operationally, but we refuse to let them stand in for success, because optimizing them directly leads to a worse company.

**Why not revenue?** Revenue is necessary and we track it seriously (§9), but it is a *lagging by-product* of delivering the North Star, not a substitute for it. Revenue can be propped up temporarily by lock-in, guilt, and switching costs — none of which mean we helped a single child. When revenue and the North Star diverge, the divergence is information: it usually means we are extracting value faster than we are creating it, and that is a problem to fix, not a result to celebrate.

**How every product decision should use it.** Before building anything, the team should be able to answer: *"What is our theory for how this moves the North Star — more families, improving more durably, in genuine engagement — without raising the anxiety guardrail?"* If a feature has no credible path to the North Star, it is at best a distraction and at worst one of the forbidden paths wearing a disguise. If a feature moves an operational metric (installs, session length) but not the North Star, we treat that as a yellow flag, not a win. The North Star is how §9's philosophy becomes a daily filter rather than a framed poster.

## 10. How the world should be different if we succeed

**For parents:** Parenting the early years becomes less lonely and less anxious. A parent has, for the first time, a private mirror that shows them their own strengths and gently points to the next small improvement — turning guilt into agency.

**For children:** More children spend their earliest years in richer, more responsive conversation — not because they were drilled, but because the adults around them became slightly more intentional. The benefit reaches the child indirectly, through the parent, which is the only ethical way it should reach a child at all.

**For society:** If the early conversational environment is genuinely improved at scale, the downstream effects — on language, on learning, on confidence — compound across a generation. Crucially, if we hold to the anti-deficit principle (§3), Melloba narrows opportunity gaps by *supporting* families rather than by telling some of them they are failing.

If Melloba succeeds and the world is *not* better for children in a way we can point to, then we have built a profitable product and failed at our purpose. Both halves of that sentence matter.

## 11. Note on exit and independence

The founding brief contemplates a possible acquisition. This document does not object to that — but it binds it.

Melloba may one day be acquired, and building for that outcome (clean code, strong IP, transferable systems, low founder dependency, regulatory compliance) is legitimate and encouraged. But the trust families place in us is **not an asset that transfers freely.** Therefore:

- The §7 privacy and child-wellbeing commitments must be built into architecture, contracts, and (where possible) legal/technical guarantees — **so that an acquirer cannot betray families even if they wanted to.** Protections that live only in our culture will not survive a change of ownership; protections that live in the system's design might.
- We will not pursue or accept an exit whose thesis depends on unlocking the family data we promised never to exploit. That is selling the one thing we swore not to sell.
- "Build to be acquirable" and "build to be trusted" must be made to point the same direction. Where they conflict, §7 governs.

An acquisition that preserves the mission is a success. An acquisition that liquidates the trust is the failure we spent the whole company avoiding.

## Appendix A — Challenges to the current direction (read this)

The founder asked for disagreement where warranted. Here is the honest version. None of these are reasons not to proceed; they are the things most likely to hurt us if left unexamined.

1. **The measurement premise is the biggest risk, and it's underspecified.** The stated MVP promises to "measure language development" and quantify "missed learning opportunities." That is scientifically hard, easy to get wrong, and dangerous to get wrong (a false "your child is behind" signal can harm a real family). Recommendation: in early versions, **describe and coach, don't diagnose or score.** Show parents *patterns* ("lots of great open-ended questions today") before claiming *measurement of the child*. Earn the right to quantify.

2. **"Measurable learning opportunities" can quietly violate §7.6 (warmth is not a metric).** The tagline instinct to make conversation *measurable* is in tension with keeping it *natural*. This tension is permanent and must be actively managed in UX (see `08-UI-UX.md`), not resolved once. Flagging it now so it isn't discovered late.

3. **The science must be de-risked from the word-gap myth immediately.** Any copy, model, or metric built around word *count* inherits a contested, deficit-loaded frame. Build on *conversational turns and contingency*. This is a technical and a marketing decision, and it is cheaper to make now than to unwind later.

4. **Recording children's audio is a regulatory and trust minefield that will shape the architecture.** COPPA (US), GDPR (EU), India's DPDP Act, and equivalents impose real constraints on processing children's data — and Melloba's core input is a child's voice. Privacy-by-architecture (edge processing, data minimization, deletion by default) should be treated as a *technical requirement in `06-Technical-Architecture.md`*, not a policy written after the fact. Getting this wrong is existential; getting it right early is a durable moat.

5. **The tech-stack assumption may fight the privacy principle.** The planned stack (React Native/Expo, Firebase, OpenAI APIs, Whisper) implies sending intimate child audio to third-party cloud services. That is in direct tension with §7.2. This does not mean the stack is wrong — it means the **data-flow and vendor decisions must be made deliberately and documented**, with on-device/edge options seriously evaluated, before implementation. Do not let a default stack silently set the privacy posture of the company.

6. **Focus is a strength; protect it.** The MVP's discipline in excluding community, gamification, marketplace, etc. is correct and rare. The risk is *reintroducing* them later under growth pressure. §8 reframes those exclusions as principled, not temporary — so that "add gamification to boost retention" is recognized as a violation, not an optimization.

7. **Beware optimizing the parent's anxiety.** Our customer (the parent) will pay for reassurance; our beneficiary (the child) needs something subtler. The most profitable version of this product and the most *good* version of this product are not the same product. Naming this now (§6, §9) is the guardrail.
